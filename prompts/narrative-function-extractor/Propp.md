# Vladimir Propp’s 31 Narrative Functions  
*from* Morphology of the Folktale, 1928

Below is the **canonical list of Propp’s narrative functions**, in their **standard order**.  
Each function is defined as an **action significant to the progress of the narrative**, independent of character psychology.  
Examples are given in **generic folktale terms** and, where useful, in **modern narrative equivalents**.

Important: For each paragraph below, the name of the Propp's function is the title (without the initial numbering)

---

## 0. Initial Situation
**Definition:** Introduction of the family, hero, or social context.  
**Example:** “There once lived a poor woodcutter and his three sons.”  
**Modern example:** Establishing the protagonist’s ordinary life before disruption.

---

## 1. Absentation
**Definition:** A family member leaves home or is absent.  
**Example:** Parents leave the house, leaving children alone.  
**Modern example:** A parent dies, disappears, or the hero lives alone.

---

## 2. Interdiction
**Definition:** A warning or command is given to the hero.  
**Example:** “Do not open this door.”  
**Modern example:** “Never talk about Fight Club.”

---

## 3. Violation of Interdiction
**Definition:** The interdiction is violated.  
**Example:** The hero opens the forbidden door.  
**Modern example:** The protagonist breaks a rule, triggering consequences.

---

## 4. Reconnaissance
**Definition:** The villain seeks information about the victim or hero.  
**Example:** A witch asks where the children live.  
**Modern example:** An antagonist spies on or investigates the hero.

---

## 5. Delivery
**Definition:** The villain receives information about the victim.  
**Example:** The witch learns the children’s location.  
**Modern example:** A traitor reveals the hero’s weakness.

---

## 6. Trickery
**Definition:** The villain attempts to deceive the victim.  
**Example:** The wolf disguises himself as the grandmother.  
**Modern example:** A con, false promise, or manipulation.

---

## 7. Complicity
**Definition:** The victim is deceived and unwittingly helps the villain.  
**Example:** Little Red Riding Hood reveals her destination.  
**Modern example:** The hero trusts the wrong person.

---

## 8. Villainy **or** Lack
**Definition:**  
- **Villainy:** The villain causes harm.  
- **Lack:** Something important is missing or desired.  
**Example (villainy):** A princess is kidnapped.  
**Example (lack):** The hero lacks money, freedom, love, or identity.

---

## 9. Mediation
**Definition:** The lack or villainy is made known; the hero is dispatched.  
**Example:** The king announces the princess is missing.  
**Modern example:** The hero learns the truth of the injustice.

---

## 10. Counteraction
**Definition:** The hero decides to act.  
**Example:** “I will rescue the princess.”  
**Modern example:** The protagonist commits to a course of action.

---

## 11. Departure
**Definition:** The hero leaves home.  
**Example:** The hero sets out on a journey.  
**Modern example:** Entering a new world, job, prison, or mindset.

---

## 12. First Function of the Donor
**Definition:** The hero is tested, interrogated, or attacked by a donor figure.  
**Example:** An old man asks the hero for food.  
**Modern example:** A mentor tests the hero’s values.

---

## 13. Hero’s Reaction
**Definition:** The hero responds to the donor’s test.  
**Example:** The hero shares his last loaf of bread.  
**Modern example:** The hero shows courage, kindness, or intelligence.

---

## 14. Receipt of a Magical Agent
**Definition:** The hero gains a magical object, helper, or power.  
**Example:** A sword, ring, horse, or spell.  
**Modern example:** Skills, knowledge, allies, or resources.

---

## 15. Guidance
**Definition:** The hero is led to the location of the object of the quest.  
**Example:** A bird guides the hero to the castle.  
**Modern example:** A map, clue, or mentor’s advice.

---

## 16. Struggle
**Definition:** The hero and villain engage in direct combat.  
**Example:** A sword fight with the dragon.  
**Modern example:** Physical, legal, psychological, or ideological confrontation.

---

## 17. Branding
**Definition:** The hero is marked or wounded.  
**Example:** A scar or ring given as proof.  
**Modern example:** Trauma, reputation, or symbolic mark.

---

## 18. Victory
**Definition:** The villain is defeated.  
**Example:** The dragon is slain.  
**Modern example:** The antagonist is exposed, overpowered, or neutralized.

---

## 19. Liquidation
**Definition:** The initial lack or villainy is resolved.  
**Example:** The princess is freed.  
**Modern example:** Justice is restored, truth revealed.

---

## 20. Return
**Definition:** The hero returns home.  
**Example:** The hero travels back from the quest.  
**Modern example:** Returning to society after transformation.

---

## 21. Pursuit
**Definition:** The hero is pursued.  
**Example:** The villain chases the hero.  
**Modern example:** Authorities, enemies, or consequences close in.

---

## 22. Rescue
**Definition:** The hero is rescued from pursuit.  
**Example:** A river or helper saves the hero.  
**Modern example:** An ally intervenes or a trap is escaped.

---

## 23. Unrecognized Arrival
**Definition:** The hero arrives home unrecognized.  
**Example:** The hero appears as a beggar.  
**Modern example:** Living under an alias or hidden identity.

---

## 24. Unfounded Claims
**Definition:** A false hero makes claims of success.  
**Example:** A pretender claims to have slain the dragon.  
**Modern example:** Someone takes credit for the hero’s work.

---

## 25. Difficult Task
**Definition:** A challenge is set for the hero.  
**Example:** Solve a riddle, perform an impossible feat.  
**Modern example:** Final test, trial, or proof of worth.

---

## 26. Solution
**Definition:** The difficult task is resolved.  
**Example:** The riddle is answered correctly.  
**Modern example:** The hero passes the final test.

---

## 27. Recognition
**Definition:** The hero is recognized by a mark or deed.  
**Example:** The scar proves true identity.  
**Modern example:** The truth about the protagonist is revealed.

---

## 28. Exposure
**Definition:** The false hero or villain is exposed.  
**Example:** The impostor is unmasked.  
**Modern example:** Corruption or deception is revealed.

---

## 29. Transfiguration
**Definition:** The hero is given a new appearance.  
**Example:** New clothes, royal garments.  
**Modern example:** Social elevation, confidence, or inner change.

---

## 30. Punishment
**Definition:** The villain is punished.  
**Example:** Execution, exile, imprisonment.  
**Modern example:** Legal consequences or moral downfall.

---

## 31. Wedding
**Definition:** The hero is rewarded (often marriage or ascension).  
**Example:** The hero marries the princess and inherits the throne.  
**Modern example:** Freedom, peace, fulfillment, or a new stable life.

---

## Notes
- Not all stories contain all 31 functions.
- Functions always appear in **this order**, though many may be omitted.
- Functions describe **what happens**, not *who does it* or *why*.
